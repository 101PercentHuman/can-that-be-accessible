## Introduction

One time at work, a colleague suggested I make what seemed like a minor, inconsequential change: say “Universal design” rather than “Accessible design”.

The end result was an answer that read:
“Universal design benefits everyone, not just people with disability.”

This was for a Slido quiz. I didn’t think too much about it. I made the change and moved on.

A few weeks later, the same colleague had some more thoughts and returned with some more notes: they disagreed that the answer was a good argument for universal design.

Their argument was simple: when we push for universal design, we should do it to primarily benefit people with disability rather than everyone as a whole. 

My argument was that the answer still had merit, because it is the argument you give to justify switching to universal design and improving accessibility.

I won’t divulge what was the final outcome. Instead, I’m left with a question that was ultimately at the heart of the minor debate: should we ever de-link disability from accessibility?

My colleague did not think so. 

When I made my argument, I implied we could.

Where do I stand now?
